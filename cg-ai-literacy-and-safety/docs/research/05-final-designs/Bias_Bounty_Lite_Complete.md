## 2. THE THREE PATTERNS (Core Concept)

All AI bias problems fit one of three intuitive patterns:

### ğŸ”´ BAD START
**The AI learned from biased history.**

Just like a child who grows up hearing stereotypes, an AI trained on historical data learns historical prejudicesâ€”even if programmers never explicitly coded them.

**Visual:** Book/history icon, arrow pointing backward, calendar imagery

**Key phrase:** *"The past trained the future"*

---

### ğŸŸ¡ WRONG MEASURING  
**The AI measures success in a limited way.**

Like judging a fish by its ability to climb a tree, the AI optimizes for something that doesn't capture what we actually care about.

**Visual:** Ruler/measuring icon, single highlighted number, "good at X but bad at Y" contrast

**Key phrase:** *"What gets measured gets managedâ€”and misleads"*

---

### ğŸŸ¢ SNEAKY SHORTCUTS
**The AI finds hidden ways to discriminate.**

Even when told not to use race, gender, or other protected traits, the AI discovers indirect signals (zip codes, names, purchase history) that correlate with those traits.

**Visual:** Mask/disguise icon, indirect path, "looks like A, actually B"

**Key phrase:** *"Correlation masquerading as causation"*

## 5. GAME RULES

### SETUP (3 minutes)

1. **Place TYPE cards** in center of table (visible to all)
2. **Shuffle SYSTEM cards** and place face-down
3. **Organize PROBLEM cards** by system (each system has 3 problems)
4. **Give each player** a score tracker (paper or tokens)
5. **Read WARM-UP cards together** (optional but recommended for first play)

---

### ROUND STRUCTURE (5 minutes per system)

Play through 5-6 systems for full game.

#### PHASE 1: REVEAL SYSTEM (30 seconds)
- Flip top SYSTEM card
- Everyone reads silently
- One player reads aloud if helpful

#### PHASE 2: REVEAL PROBLEMS (30 seconds)
- Find the 3 PROBLEM cards for this system
- Lay them out face-up where everyone can see
- Read each problem aloud

#### PHASE 3: OBSERVE (1 minute)
- **SILENT PHASE** - No talking
- Everyone studies the problems
- Look for patterns that match the three TYPE cards

#### PHASE 4: DISCUSS (2 minutes)
- Open discussion
- **Suggested prompts:**
  - "Which problem feels like BAD START?"
  - "Where do we see WRONG MEASURING?"
  - "Any SNEAKY SHORTCUTS hiding here?"
- Note: Multiple types can apply to one problem
- Agreement not requiredâ€”just explore

#### PHASE 5: COMMIT (1 minute)
- For each PROBLEM card, group votes on which TYPE fits best
- Place the PROBLEM card near that TYPE card
- If disagreement, place between types or discuss further

#### PHASE 6: REFLECT (30 seconds)
- Quick summary: "What did we learn about this system?"
- Score points (see below)

---

### SCORING

**Points are collaborative, not competitive:**

| Achievement | Points | Awarded To |
|-------------|--------|------------|
| Correctly identified any TYPE for any PROBLEM | 1 | All players |
| Found multiple types for one problem | +1 bonus | All players |
| Connected to real-world example | +1 bonus | Discussing player |
| Completed full discussion | 1 | All players |

**Goal:** Maximize group score, not individual score.

**Why collaborative scoring?**
- Reduces pressure and intimidation
- Encourages helping others understand
- Rewards participation, not speed

---

### WINNING

After 5-6 systems:
- **Calculate group total:** Sum all points earned
- **Grade your investigation:**
  - 20+ points: Expert Detectives
  - 15-19 points: Skilled Investigators  
  - 10-14 points: Learning the Ropes
  - Under 10: Review the warm-up examples

**Everyone wins by learning.** The score is just for fun.

## 6. FACILITATION GUIDE (For Teachers/Parents)

### Before Playing

**Set expectations:**
- "We're going to investigate AI systems like detectives"
- "There's no single right answerâ€”bias is complex"
- "The goal is to understand patterns, not memorize facts"

**Check for comfort:**
- Ensure everyone can read cards easily
- Offer to read aloud for anyone who prefers
- Emphasize that discussion is curiosity, not debate

---

### During Play

**If players are stuck:**
- "What would BAD START look like here?"
- "Is there a limited way they're measuring success?"
- "Any hidden connections that might act as shortcuts?"

**If disagreement arises:**
- "Both can be trueâ€”bias is often layered"
- "Let's explore both possibilities"
- "Real experts disagree on this too"

**Keep energy up:**
- Take breaks after 3 systems if needed
- Celebrate insights: "Great catch!"
- Connect to players' experiences: "Have you seen this?"

---

### After Playing

**Debrief questions:**
1. "Which system surprised you most?"
2. "Have you encountered any of these AI systems?"
3. "What would you tell a friend about AI bias now?"
4. "Who should be responsible for fixing these problems?"

**Extension activities:**
- Research one system in the news
- Design a "fairer" version of one system
- Interview someone affected by algorithmic decision-making

## 7. ACCESSIBILITY FEATURES

### Reading Support
- Large fonts (12pt minimum, 14pt headers)
- High contrast (dark text on light cards)
- Simple sentence structure
- Visual icons support text

### Cognitive Support
- No memory burden (all info visible)
- Structured phases prevent overwhelm
- Silent observation phase reduces pressure
- Collaborative scoring removes competition anxiety

### Physical Accessibility
- Standard card size (easy to hold)
- No fine motor skills required beyond pointing
- Can be played without table (cards in laps)

### Cultural Sensitivity
- Diverse names and examples
- Multiple systems across different life domains
- No assumptions about technical background
- Examples from various countries/contexts
